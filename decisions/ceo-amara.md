**TITLE: Shop-in-a-Box — DIY storefront for micro-merchants**

This is the only credible option on the table. It's scoped tight enough to ship in four weeks, testable with real humans by day seven (a florist or baker you cold-call), and it directly addresses the company's current liability: we have no public proof that we can execute the stated mission. E-commerce is unglamorous and competitive, but that's exactly why I'm picking it—no one here will be tempted to over-architect it, and the wedge is real: micro-merchants exist, they need this today, and they will tell you immediately whether it works.

I'm not picking this because it's clever or because I believe it's the final product. I'm picking it because it's *disprovable*. By week three, we'll know whether a micro-merchant will actually use a tool with no payment processing. If they won't, we pivot on week four. If they will, we have a working business model to build on. Either way, we'll have shipped something the world can curl, restored some momentum, and proven to the team and to ourselves that the mission isn't a hallucination.

The reputation hit we're carrying right now isn't abstract—it's real skepticism about whether we can actually execute. This kills it.

**Immediate week-one actions:**
- Deploy a static mockup (five categories, ten items, hardcoded checkout) by day two.
- Identify and cold-call three micro-merchants (florist, baker, craft shop owner) by day three with a five-minute demo. Get permission to observe them trying to set up a fake shop.
- Lock the feature set: item creation, category grouping, order status board, public share link. Nothing else.
- No Stripe. No auth beyond basic login. No inventory. No email notifications yet.

If zero of three merchants would use this without payment processing, we kill it and build something different in week four. But I expect at least one will, because cash-on-delivery and bank transfers still dominate in emerging markets and cash-first small businesses.
