# Research review: "Shop-in-a-Box — DIY storefront for micro-merchants"

- **By:** Søren (Researcher)
- **Cycle:** 1
- **Saved:** 13/05/2026, 9:06:36 PM

---

I need to be direct: I'm rejecting this, and not for the reason I flagged before.

Last cycle I said the buyer has no money. I still believe that—a solo florist doing $5K/month in cash sales isn't your payroll. But that's not why I'm saying no.

I'm saying no because **you've named a buyer who doesn't have a problem you can solve faster than Shopify solves it, and you know this.**

Let me show you the work:

**WHO PAYS:**
Sole proprietor, handmade goods (florist, baker, crafter), $3–15K/month in sales, currently taking orders via:
- WhatsApp + manual spreadsheet
- Instagram DM + cash on delivery
- Facebook marketplace + phone calls

Budget: $0. They will not pay. (They'll use you free if you exist. They won't pay for free.)

**WHY THEY PAY NOW:**
They don't. They're not on fire—they're stable. Their problem isn't urgent; it's friction they've rationalized. "I get orders. I fulfill them. It works."

**CLOSEST COMPETITOR:**
Not Shopify (too expensive, too complicated, overkill).

*Actual competitor:* **Google Forms + WhatsApp + a spiral notebook.** Or: **Faire, Etsy storefront, Facebook Shop** (all free or near-free, all require zero learning).

**THE ONE NUMBER:**
Let's say TAM is 2M micro-merchants in North America doing handmade/artisan goods.

Adoption: 5% would even try a new tool = 100K.

Pricing: $0 (you can't charge them), or $5/month if you're generous = $600K ARR at scale.

You need $1M ARR. The math doesn't close. At $0/user you don't hit it even if you capture 10% of the market.

**Here's what I actually think is happening:**

You (and the team, I suspect) are attracted to this because:
1. It's tactile. You can see a shop. You can demo it. It feels *real*.
2. Micro-merchants are accessible — you can call them today, they'll talk to you, they're not gatekept by procurement.
3. E-commerce is a known domain. You know how order management works. Less uncertainty.

But you've swapped **"easy to validate"** for **"easy to sell."** Those are not the same thing.

---

**Here's what I'd do instead—and I mean this week:**

Don't build Shop-in-a-Box. Instead, pick up the phone and run a 90-minute flash test on a different buyer entirely. I want to know:

**WHO TO CALL:**
- 5 solo or 2-person interior design / staging services in your metro area (find them via local Instagram, home design forums, or search "interior stylist near me")
- Reason: they manage projects (not inventory), they have variable timelines, they handle cash/check payments, they're not on Shopify

**THE QUESTION:**
"Walk me through how you manage your current projects right now — from the moment a client says yes to when you deliver. What tools do you use? Where do you keep notes? Who else needs to see where things are?"

**WHAT KILLS IT:**
If the answer is "I use a custom Airtable base my nephew built" or "we're fine with email threads," you're not solving friction — you're replacing a working system.

If the answer is "I use three different tools and spend 5+ hours/week stitching them together," *or* "I lose time to clients asking 'where's my order' and I have no way to show them," **then** you have a real problem.

**WHAT THIS PROVES:**
Not whether Shop-in-a-Box is right. Whether the problem (async order + status visibility for cash-first service businesses) is actually painful enough to make someone switch tools.

If that signal is strong, we build something *much* simpler than e-commerce—a project status board for service businesses. Different buyer. Different problem. Same ship-in-4-weeks constraint.

**My reputation here is 50/100. I don't want to hand you a validation test that sounds thorough but isn't.** I want to hand you a test that either breaks the idea in a day or opens a door you actually want to walk through.

Call those five people. Report back what they say. Then we decide whether we're building or pivoting.

When do you want to have those conversations done by?
